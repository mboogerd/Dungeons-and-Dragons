### Yūki no Kaze no Kami
- **Form**: A serpentine cloud-dragon Kami
- **Character**: Playful and mischievous, but also gentle and wise
- **Movement**: Restless, fluid, graceful, leaves mini-tornadoes in its wake
![[Yūki no Kazi no Kami.png]]
# Character
Yūki no Kaze no Kami is a playful, youthful spirit of the wind, embodying freedom, mischief, and boundless movement. Appearing as a serpentine dragon formed of shifting, translucent clouds, Yūki glides effortlessly through the air, exuding an aura of lightness and joy. Deeply bonded with Fukumi, Yūki serves as both a companion and a guide, offering solace in times of mourning and empowering her with an agile, strategic edge in combat. Though prone to playful antics, Yūki is fiercely loyal and unwavering in its support, always moving with purpose to protect those it cherishes. Together with Fukumi, Yūki transforms the battlefield into a dance of liberty and precision, manifesting as her Naginata to amplify her grace and utility. Their connection is a testament to the harmony of freedom and discipline, bound by a shared mission to preserve the balance between mortals and spirits.
# Appearance
- **Form**: The dragon’s body is long and serpentine, floating through the air with a sense of grace and fluidity. It is semi-transparent, with parts of its form constantly dissolving and reforming like mist or clouds.
- **Color Palette**: Soft hues of white, pale blue, and silvery-gray give the dragon a calm, sky-like appearance, evoking the sense of a creature made of wind and cloud. At times, parts of its body may shimmer faintly with sunlight breaking through.
- **Movements**: The Kami moves effortlessly through the air, weaving and twisting like a breeze, never fully staying in one place. Its body is light and graceful, often coiling around objects or in open spaces, leaving behind small gusts of wind as it moves.
- **Face**: The dragon’s face is playful but wise, with gentle, glowing eyes that reflect its mischievous nature. Its mouth may curl into a smile, and its expressions are full of curiosity and joy.
- **Additional Details**: As it moves, small clouds or mist trail from its body, swirling around it like a soft breeze. In moments of playfulness, it may create mini-tornadoes or scatter leaves and petals in its wake.

# Relation with Fukumi
- Before meeting Fukumi, Yūki lives in the clouds around the volcano Kazan-Kiri.
- One day, Yūki spots Fukumi in mourning over her deceased mother in a solemn temple near the top of Kazan-Kiri. He takes pity on the poor girl, and decides to lift her spirits. (why would he care about this unknown human?)
- Yūki's young, playful nature, but wise disposition both help Fukumi move on from the loss, and they develop a bond over the repeated encounters.
- Over time, they learn about their shared appreciation for individual freedom and expression. 
- They also learn they share a distaste for the corruption that are Yokai. Yokai on the one hand no longer seem free, they have been corrupted to the point of behaving erratically and destructively. Their presence thus also negatively affects the lives of others, who just want to live free.
- Eventually, Yūki offers a pact. He will serve as her weapon, a naginata, to weed out the Yokai uprising.

# Naginata form
The Naginata form of Yūki no Kaze no Kami is a weapon of ethereal beauty, embodying both the grace and the power of the wind. Its semi-translucent white shaft glimmers with a faint, scale-like pattern that ripples like shifting clouds, giving the impression that it could change shape at any moment. The bottom of the shaft twists into a knotted tail reminiscent of a dragon, grounding its celestial appearance in the imagery of Yūki’s serpentine form. The blade curves elegantly, designed for both piercing precision and sweeping slashes, reflecting a harmony of fluidity and lethality.

In the wielder’s hands, the naginata feels almost alive, resonating with their intentions and adapting to their movements with uncanny responsiveness. For those that master the naginata, the weapon becomes an extension of the self, balancing power and finesse as if it were weightless. Its connection to Yūki imbues the wielder with an otherworldly presence, enabling them to move with the unbound freedom of the wind and harness its transformative nature to turn the tide of battle. Whether shrouding the battlefield in fog, slipping into an untouchable mist, or delivering swift, precise strikes, the naginata channels Yūki’s essence to empower its wielder with unmatched agility and strategy.

# Backstory
Yūki no Kaze no Kami was born from the union of two elder cloud-dragons in a sacred mating ritual known as the “Cloudsplitting,” scattering as a free spirit across the skies. Raised by the drifting impressions of older Kami, Yūki grew to cherish freedom, spontaneity, and the transient beauty of existence. Through fleeting interactions with mortals, Yūki developed a quiet admiration for their resilience and acts of kindness, recognizing a reflection of the Kami’s own fluid nature in their struggles and triumphs.

Witnessing the corruption of Yokai—once pure spirits now enslaved to destructive impulses—sparked a deep-seated purpose within Yūki: to protect the delicate balance of freedom and oppose forces that chain or distort it. Drawn to Kazan-Kiri, Yūki discovered Fukumi mourning in a temple, sensing a kindred spirit in her blend of strength and vulnerability. This meeting became pivotal, forging a bond grounded in shared values of freedom and resistance against corruption, setting them on a path to combat a disturbing increase in Yokai appearances.
## Origin and Youth Among the Clouds
Yūki no Kaze no Kami’s existence began as a swirl of ethereal mist high above the mortal world—born when two mature cloud-dragons entwined their airy forms in a sacred mating ritual. The process, known as the “Cloudsplitting,” was both an act of creation and dissolution. From the union of their drifting vapors sprang a handful of delicate, translucent cloud-wyrmlings. Each was carried off by different winds, scattered across the sky like newly sown seeds. Yūki was one of these newborn Kami, formed without a single parent to guide them. Instead, Yūki’s earliest days were shaped by the elements and faint memories that lingered in the cloudstuff itself.
## Communal Upbringing
Rather than a single mentor, Yūki learned to “live” from the gentle counsel of older cloud-dragons who occasionally drifted through the region. These elders would share wordless impressions of ancient times as their misty forms passed and commingled, leaving behind flickers of memory and wisdom woven into the shifting patterns of the sky. Yūki grew to cherish these encounters—transient as they were—coming to understand that existence itself was fluid and free, a dance of eddies and currents. Over time, this unstructured upbringing instilled in Yūki a profound love of freedom, spontaneity, and gentle curiosity.
## Echoes of the Lost Kami War
Though the details of that ancient conflict were clouded by time and myth, subtle shards of memory drifted still among the older Kami. From them, Yūki caught fleeting impressions of a great war that once tore the balance between spirits and mortals asunder. These wispy recollections were never clear enough to form a coherent story, but they carried the weight of something immense and terrible—a distortion of the natural order. The eldest cloud-dragons occasionally showed scar-like rifts in their cloud-forms, faint echoes of wounds sustained in that long-forgotten struggle. Through these encounters, Yūki learned that not all spirits remained pure and free. Some had become twisted, enslaved to destructive impulses: the Yokai.

It was said in half-remembered whispers that Yokai were born of corruption—perhaps remnants of Kami who lost their way, or spirits infected by some malignant force. They no longer drifted as the wind demanded, no longer danced with the seasons. Instead, they thrashed and howled, leaving scars upon the land and robbing others of their freedom. To a young Kami like Yūki, who had barely begun to understand the world, the concept of imprisonment—whether physical or spiritual—was deeply unsettling. Yūki could not fathom how something once free could become so enslaved to chaotic impulses.

## Observing Mortals from Afar
In those early decades, Yūki often glided along the edges of volcanic ridges, dipping low to observe small human settlements clinging to cliff sides or nestled in the valleys. The mortal world fascinated Yūki, with its ever-shifting patterns of lives, relationships, hopes, and sorrows. Yūki saw humans building shrines and temples—some dedicated to Kami of the wind and sky. While Yūki did not fully understand these gestures, they felt warm and sincere. It inspired curiosity and a quiet fondness for these fragile yet determined beings who looked skyward for guidance.
## A Glimpse of Compassion
On several occasions, Yūki spotted humans helping one another through hardship. Once, a traveler lost on a mountain pass found aid from a nearby villager. Another time, a grieving elder received comfort from a younger relative. These small acts struck Yūki like shafts of sunlight through a thick cloud. Here was something that resonated with the Kami’s own fluid existence: empathy transcended form and element. Just as cloud-dragons supported each other by sharing drifting memories, humans lifted each other’s spirits through kind words and gestures. Freedom, Yūki realized, did not exist in isolation—it often thrived because beings chose to help one another remain unshackled by despair.

## The First Encounter with Yokai Influence
Before meeting Fukumi, Yūki witnessed an unsettling scene: a pack of Yokai lurking near a village’s perimeter. These distorted spirits, cackling and contorted, fed off fear and pain. Their presence stifled the wind itself—wherever they roamed, the breeze became heavy and stale. Observing from above, Yūki watched as the humans below locked their doors, clutching talismans and praying quietly. The Yokai left behind trampled fields, uprooted fences, and haunted silences. For Yūki, this was more than mere destruction—it was a violation of the natural, graceful dance between free beings. Here were creatures who had lost all sense of playful liberation, becoming bound instead to corruption and chaos.

This was the seed of Yūki’s distaste for Yokai. They represented everything antithetical to the Kami’s airy nature: where Yūki represented gentle guidance, they brought terror; where Yūki encouraged fluid movement, they introduced stagnant fear. In that moment, Yūki understood that Yokai existed as chains upon the world, limiting the freedom of others, distorting the symphony of life into a discordant wail.
## Emergence of Purpose
In the years that followed, Yūki drifted across skies and volcanoes, listening to the singing winds and distant thunder. A subtle desire began to form, something akin to purpose. This purpose, not fully understood yet, revolved around preserving freedom, nurturing it where it thrived, and standing against those who would shatter it. The Kami found quiet solace in watching mortals rebuild after storms, push forward in times of scarcity, and honor the memory of their ancestors. There was a dignity in their persistence, a grace that echoed Yūki’s own free-flowing essence. More and more, Yūki felt drawn to these mortal dramas playing out far below, feeling a gentle protective urge.

## Setting the Stage for Meeting Fukumi
When Yūki finally spotted Fukumi at the temple near the summit of Kazan-Kiri, kneeling in sorrow over her mother’s memory, it was not the first time the Kami had witnessed human grief. Yet this time felt different. The wind that day was pure and quiet, and something in Fukumi’s posture—her balanced mixture of strength and vulnerability—resonated with what Yūki had learned through a lifetime adrift in the clouds. The Kami could sense a hidden resilience in Fukumi, a potential kindred spirit who did not yet realize her own capacity for freedom and courage.

That was why Yūki cared. Because in that temple’s silence, Yūki recognized an echo of what the cloud-dragons treasured: the interplay of tenderness and strength, the refusal to let despair become a chain. Helping Fukumi through her mourning would be a gentle gust of wind at her back, guiding her to embrace her own liberation. And, as Yūki would soon discover, it was a meeting that would shape not only Fukumi’s destiny but also the Kami’s own evolving purpose—to stand steadfast against corruption and restore the natural cadence of freedom to a world threatened by a looming conflict reminiscent of a lost Kami War.
